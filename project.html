The Effect of Working from Home on Productivity.

Introduction

The aim of my project will be to examine the relationship between working from home and levels of labour productivity. Throughout the pandemic, many industries changed from being office-based to encouraging their workers to work from home. The changes in the level of people working from home can be seen below.

After the Coronavirus pandemic hit, the only category of working from home to increase was ‘mainly’, indicating that the proportion of people using home as their primary place of work increased. All other categories decreased after 2019, again pointing towards a major change in the percentage of those working from home.

This being the case, I was curious to see whether the level of economy-wide output per hour changed at all. It could have improved as the time spent commuting was reduced and so people may be better rested, or it may have decreased as there is unlikely to be the same moderation of work at home than there is in an office. Perhaps there will be no difference at all, as the proportion of those who are now working from home make up such a small proportion of the labour force as a whole it won’t change much.

This chart consists of UK and US data from different sources. The UK data comes from an ONS API, and the US data from FRED data hosted on GitHub. Both are easily downloadable and this chart should be very repeatable.

We can see from this chart that after an initial fall in output per hour, both countries recovered and saw an increase in output per hour in early-mid 2020. The US recovered more wuickly than the UK, and both the dip and subsequent rise in output per hour was larger.

A major issue with this project and analysing the data was that when looking at the direct productivity data, it is hard to say whether a dip in productivity over the period of the pandemic was caused by working from home, or people simply not working. In order to combat this, I have included countries such as New Zealand, who have published data regarding how many people were working from home and how many weren’t working at all due to restrictions. I have also attempted to focus on the time periods where the furlough scheme wasn’t in place, as well as look at a country similar in demographic to the UK but that didn’t offer as extensive a furlough scheme, such as the US, in order to hopefully gauge the isolated impact of working from home.

It is difficult to know the success of these attempts. Due to this, I also included data from a survey of people analysing their own productivity when working from home and not.
